Don't these IR infrared led arrays need to flash between camera frames? Can I do that with the IR lights you have shown me?
Short answer: Yes — PiTrac does need the IR lights to pulse between camera frames, and no — the off‑the‑shelf IR floodlights I listed earlier cannot do that. They are always on and cannot be synchronized with the cameras.

Let me break it down cleanly so you know exactly what’s required and what your options are.

🔦 Why PiTrac needs pulsed IR lighting
High‑speed global‑shutter cameras need extremely short, bright bursts of IR light so they can:

Freeze the ball in motion

Avoid motion blur

Capture spin dots clearly

Reduce background noise

This is exactly how commercial launch monitors work — they strobe the IR LEDs in sync with the camera exposure.

If the IR light is always on, you get:

Washed‑out frames

Lower contrast

Blurry ball edges

Poor spin detection

So yes, pulsing is required for best performance.

❌ Why off‑the‑shelf IR floodlights won’t work for pulsing
The IR illuminators you can buy on Amazon:

Are designed for security cameras

Run continuously

Use internal drivers that cannot be switched rapidly

Cannot sync with a Raspberry Pi or camera trigger

Cannot flash at high frequency

Even if you tried to pulse them with an external switch, the internal circuitry would prevent fast strobing.

So they’re great for general IR lighting, but not for a launch monitor.

🟢 What does work for PiTrac pulsed IR lighting
You need bare IR LED boards that can be driven directly by a MOSFET or LED driver controlled by the Pi.

These boards:

Have no internal driver

Can be pulsed at microsecond speeds

Can sync with camera exposure

Are used by most DIY launch monitor builders

Examples of the right type of LED modules:

850 nm IR LED panels (raw LED arrays)

3W–5W IR emitters

1W IR star LEDs

Custom LED bars designed for strobing

These require:

A MOSFET

A current‑limited LED driver

A GPIO trigger

A 12 V power supply

Let me unpack that in a way that’s actually useful when you’re buying parts.

🔦 1. PiTrac doesn’t care about LED wattage — it cares about IR intensity
Wattage on IR LEDs is just a rough indicator of how much light they can produce.
What PiTrac actually needs is:

Very bright IR output

Delivered in a very short pulse

Synchronized with the camera exposure

This is what freezes the golf ball in motion and reveals spin dots.

So the real requirement is pulse brightness, not wattage.

🔍 2. Typical LED wattages that work well

📌 3. What PiTrac actually needs in practice
For a dual‑camera PiTrac, the sweet spot is:

➡ 6–12 watts of total IR LED power per camera
This can be achieved with:

Two 3W LEDs

One 5W + one 3W

A 10W IR LED module

Any of these combinations work as long as:

They are raw LEDs (no built‑in driver)

They can be pulsed via MOSFET

They are cooled properly

⚡ 4. Why wattage isn’t the limiting factor
The real bottlenecks are:

A. Pulse duration
PiTrac uses extremely short pulses (hundreds of microseconds).
Higher wattage LEDs help because they deliver more photons in that tiny window.

B. LED driver current
A 3W LED typically runs at ~700 mA.
A 5W LED runs at ~1A.
Your driver must match this.

C. Heat dissipation
Higher wattage LEDs need heatsinks, even when pulsed.

🔦 Overview: What connects to what
Here’s the big picture in one sentence:

The 12V power supply feeds the MOSFET → the MOSFET feeds the LED driver → the LED driver feeds the LEDs.

That’s the whole chain.

Now let’s break it down step‑by‑step.

🧩 1. Power Supply → MOSFET (Input Side)
Your ALITOVE 12V 5A supply has:

+V (12V positive)

–V (ground)

You run those into the MOSFET module’s input terminals:

Code
ALITOVE +V  →  MOSFET VIN+
ALITOVE –V  →  MOSFET VIN–
This gives the MOSFET the power it will switch on/off.

🧩 2. MOSFET (Output Side) → LED Driver (Input Side)
The MOSFET has an output pair of terminals:

Code
MOSFET VOUT+  →  LED Driver +IN
MOSFET VOUT–  →  LED Driver –IN
This means the MOSFET is controlling the power going into the LED driver.

This is the correct way to pulse high‑power LEDs.

🧩 3. LED Driver (Output Side) → LEDs (Series Wiring)
Your constant‑current LED driver has:

+OUT

–OUT

You wire your two pre‑wired LEDs in series like this:

Code
LED Driver +OUT  →  LED #1 (+ red wire)
LED #1 (black wire –)  →  LED #2 (+ red wire)
LED #2 (black wire –)  →  LED Driver –OUT
That’s it — two LEDs in a simple chain.

No soldering.
Just screw terminals and the wires already attached to the LEDs.

🧩 4. Raspberry Pi → MOSFET (Control Signal)
The MOSFET has a signal input (usually a 3‑pin header):

SIG (signal)

+5V (power for the MOSFET board)

GND (ground)

You connect:

Code
Pi GPIO pin (your strobe pin)  →  MOSFET SIG
Pi 5V                          →  MOSFET +5V
Pi GND                         →  MOSFET GND
This lets the Pi pulse the LEDs in sync with the cameras.
